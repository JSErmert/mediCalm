The patient reports a history of multiple musculoskeletal injuries and progressive neuromuscular symptoms over the past approximately four years. The initial event involved a traumatic shoulder dislocation (likely anterior) that was manually reduced without formal medical evaluation or rehabilitation. Subsequently, the patient experienced a second dislocation of the same shoulder in the opposite direction, which self-reduced. Since these events, the patient describes ongoing instability of the shoulder joint, suggesting possible multidirectional instability. In the absence of structured rehabilitation, the patient developed compensatory movement patterns, including increased reliance on the latissimus dorsi and other global stabilizers.

In the period following the initial shoulder injury, the patient reports adopting a habitual sleep position involving prone lying with the head rotated to the left and the cervical spine placed in sustained rotation for extended durations nightly. Over time, this likely contributed to asymmetrical cervical loading and increased baseline tension in the cervical musculature. Approximately three years ago, the patient experienced a significant cervical event while in a forward-flexed posture (similar to a slouched “phone” position), combined with deep inhalation and active cervical muscle contraction. During this movement, the patient felt a sudden pop localized to the right posterior cervical region, followed immediately by pain and neurological-type symptoms, including altered sensation, burning, and impaired coordination/strength. Since that event, the patient reports persistent point tenderness in the posterior cervical spine and a subjective forward head positional change.

Following the cervical incident, the patient entered a chronic phase characterized by altered cervical mechanics, increased muscular guarding, and changes in breathing patterns. The patient then experienced a significant overuse event involving approximately three hours of latissimus-focused resistance training (pulldowns) followed by heavy rowing (185 lbs, novel load). This resulted in the onset of right-sided posterior rib pain near the costovertebral region, consistent with intercostal and/or thoracic joint irritation. Symptoms included pain with breathing and movement, as well as progressive development of nerve-like sensations.

Subsequently, the patient reports a notable exacerbation during a car-related episode involving seated compression, bracing, and altered breathing mechanics. This episode reproduced and intensified symptoms, including burning, tightening, and radiating sensations extending from the posterior rib region upward into the cervical spine and jaw. Following this, the patient began experiencing temporomandibular symptoms described as jaw instability, altered coordination, and a sensation of subluxation. These symptoms appear to be positionally and mechanically influenced.

At present, the patient describes a chronic condition involving right posterior rib pain, intermittent burning and tightening sensations with a neuropathic quality, and symptom exacerbation with certain postures, particularly prolonged sitting or bracing (e.g., in a vehicle). The patient notes significant symptomatic relief with rib mobility exercises, controlled breathing, and positional decompression, suggesting a strong mechanical and neuromuscular component. Overall, the clinical picture is consistent with a history of untreated shoulder instability, followed by cervical spine irritation with neurological features, and subsequent development of compensatory thoracic/rib dysfunction and secondary cervicofacial involvement.
